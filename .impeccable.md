## Design Context

### Users
**Candidates** — Tutor applicants (likely early-career educators or graduates) completing a 10-minute voice interview from home on a laptop. They may be nervous. The interface should reduce friction and anxiety, not add to it.

**Admins** — Cuemath hiring staff reviewing batches of interview assessments on desktop. They need to scan, compare, and make decisions quickly. Density and clarity matter more than delight.

### Brand Personality
**Calm. Precise. Trustworthy.**

This is an evaluation tool, not a consumer product. It should feel like a well-run process — quiet confidence, no unnecessary decoration, nothing that distracts from the task. The candidate should feel respected; the admin should feel in control.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Tone**: Minimal, dark by default. Restrained surfaces, generous negative space, sharp typographic hierarchy.
- **Theme**: Dark mode as primary. Light mode as secondary (supported but not the design starting point).
- **Identity**: Own product identity — not Cuemath-branded. No inherited brand colors or assets.
- **References**: The composed stillness of Linear's interface. The information density of Vercel's dashboards. The typographic confidence of Stripe's documentation.
- **Anti-references**: Generic HR/ATS tools (Greenhouse, Lever). Overly playful ed-tech aesthetics. Glassmorphism dashboards. Anything that looks like a SaaS landing page template.

### Design Principles
1. **Quiet authority** — The interface earns trust through precision, not decoration. Every element should feel deliberate.
2. **Reduce, don't add** — When in doubt, remove. White space is a feature. The content is the interface.
3. **Hierarchy through type** — Use size, weight, and spacing to create structure. Avoid visual crutches like colored borders, badges everywhere, or card nesting.
4. **Respect the context** — Candidates are in a high-stakes moment. Admins are doing repetitive evaluation. Design for their emotional state, not yours.
5. **Dark-native** — Design for dark first. Colors, contrast, and surfaces should feel native to dark backgrounds, not light-mode designs inverted.
